What is CASA? (2/x)

"Every child placed in foster care must have court oversight to ensure that both the child’s and the parents’ legal rights are protected until the child reaches a safe, permanent home, whether the child is returned to their home of origin or adopted.

"The first CASA Program was founded in 1977 by a Juvenile Court judge in Seattle, Washington who felt that he was making critical decisions about children’s lives without adequate information. He began recruiting volunteers from the community to work one-on-one with children in foster care. The volunteer advocate, or “CASA,” is appointed by a juvenile court judge to a child in foster care. The CASA then gets to know their assigned child, gathers information from everyone in the child’s life (including the child’s biological parents, foster parents, medical and mental health professionals, social workers, therapists, teachers, and attorneys) and prepares a written report giving the judge in the child’s court case detailed information about the situation and the child’s needs."

Head of CASA Montgomery, Francha
started in the 70s

What do we do that works? (1/x)

  • topic goodfeels- fewer links in the chain to human impact
  • a nontechnical pitch brings in a solid set of contributors
  • weekly stakeholder sync keeps inspiration fresh- hearing frontline stories
  • Live-”blog” stakeholder meetings into a slack thread and contributors looking for work sometimes get started fixing it before you make it into a github issue
This is custom advice. It may not alpply to your situation. Purposeful-feeling volunteering writing 911 dispatch software is too direct for me, writing code that sells shoes is too indirect for me, this is the sweet spot. Meeting the people who use the software - getting to one link away from the action

What do we do that works? (2/x)

  • Office hours on the same day as stakeholder sync, to follow up on making github issues for requested features while they’re fresh in mind
  • Weekly “office hours” to hang out & cowork - it really drives the project forward
  • Accept the slow/lean times in between scheduled conferences
  • Hacktoberfest: tickets prep, doing tickets...
  • Multiple maintainers, all mostly in agreement
  • Fast PR merge with friendly comments!!!
Stakeholders send us emails with usage questions Typeform bug reporter that auto-posts to private maintainers slack channel Weekly calls w/ stakeholders (to keep them invested, us motivated, and produce feature requests)

What do we do that works? (3/x)

  • Slack: R4G slack as central location - easy for current maintainers & multi-project r4g contributors, crosstalk advice between different project maintainers (+ removing moderator role from maintainers)
  • Slack: active public channel
  • Slack: maintainer private channel
  • Slack: bot channel for PRs, issues, bugsnag errors, and deploys to show up in

What do we do that works? (4/x)

  • R4G negotiating for OSS credits for *all* projects (heroku, twilio, sendinblue, azure, etc)
  • Leveraging Hacktoberfest (very little spam for us the small fry)
  • Easy setup via Docker (optional)
  • A big community of technically capable people who can respond to urgent needs even if they don’t otherwise contribute often (and a way to reach them)

What do we do that works? (5/x)

  • Keep everything close together. Eschew extra logins (to another project tracker, to CI) etc- reduces onboarding issues => more contributors.
  • A big community of technically capable people who can respond to urgent needs even if they don’t otherwise contribute often (and a way to reach them)
  • Encourage no-github-issue PRs for adding testing, upgrading deps, documentation, other dev chores (AND make chore issues so people know what needs doing)
  • Checklist for deploying + Checklist includes stakeholder communications
  • Weekly deploys, even if there is very little to deploy - keeping a cadence is key- so if you need to skip a week, it’s not a big deal

What do we do that works? (6/x)

Use available tooling!

  • Basics: PR templates, Issue templates (multiple & custom)
  • New issues auto-assign to the github project
  • Free CI however we can get it (farewell Travis, hello Github Actions)
  • Split CI into multiple jobs
  • Github bot to auto-tag with what's in the PR (ruby, js, db) for easier reviewing prioritzation
  • (controversial) Fast PR merge!!! Draws in contributors, keeps them engaged, and encourage follow-up PRs
  • To enable fast merge, qa -> staging -> prod 3 envs for safety (+ extra db/data migration testing env)
New issues auto-assign to the github project - trial and error, never found the docs on it, but url makes sense

A note on purpose

Be aware that your purpose as a project (may) be more than making a working tool for some users. Our purpose is also to help people learn how to contribute, and I consider it equally important. I could theoretically (for a while at least) build it all myself but that’s not what I want (time, flexibility, mental health) This may not be you! If you are big/popular project with more contributors than you want & a pack of paid maintainers, then this is not your problem (but turning the contributors that you have *into* the contributors that you *want* can work!) If you want more contributors than you have, you have to consciously and conspicuously invest in making sure that people know what to do. Office hours for people who want a human face, slack reminders for people who want to show up and figure it out, a good readme so people *can* figure it out, issues that explain what needs doing using url&screenshot&login rather than domain terms that new contributors won’t know
